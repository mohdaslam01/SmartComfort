1. From Static Set-Points to Living Comfort Models

Traditional standards assume ‚Äúaverage‚Äù people in ideal conditions. The occupant-centric framework instead treats comfort as a dynamic, personal, and learnable experience.

Framework Overview

In the legacy model, comfort is calculated from periodic measurements of temperature and humidity, combined with population-based norms. A single ‚Äúcomfort range‚Äù is then applied to everyone in a room, a building, or even a region.

In reality, comfort depends on clothing, activity level, health, culture, age, and many subtle factors. This mismatch forces people in every country to manually adjust thermostats, fans, windows, and blinds ‚Äî wasting energy and creating frustration.

The new framework upgrades this approach by making comfort:

  • Personalized ‚Äì responsive to each occupant, not an average.
  • Continuous ‚Äì updated online, not fixed in a table.
  • Collaborative ‚Äì decisions shared between humans and intelligent systems.

Key building blocks of the occupant-centric framework:

  • Multi-sensor fusion combines ambient measurements (temperature, humidity, CO‚ÇÇ, light, air speed) with wearable data (skin temperature, heart rate) and behavioral cues (movement, occupancy).
  • Active feedback ‚Äì people report ‚Äútoo warm / too cool / just right‚Äù via phones, wearables, or simple interfaces.
  • Online modeling ‚Äì AI algorithms continuously learn each person‚Äôs neutral comfort zone and update it as conditions change.
  • Human-in-the-loop control ‚Äì the system acts, but occupants remain in control and can override or guide its decisions.

2. Neutral Comfort Zone & Adaptive Environmental Control

Comfort is no longer a single number. It becomes a zone that respects human diversity while reducing energy use.

Comfort & Control

Using sensor data and occupant feedback, the system estimates a neutral comfort zone ‚Äî the range where people feel neither too warm nor too cold. This zone can differ between individuals, groups, times of day, and even cultures.

Within this zone, temperature, humidity, light, and air movement can float gently instead of being held at a fixed value. This approach:

  • Reduces unnecessary HVAC cycling and energy use (potential savings of 20‚Äì40%).
  • Prevents over-cooling and over-heating, common in offices and public buildings worldwide.
  • Supports comfort for sensitive groups: children, elders, and those with specific health needs.

Intelligent actuation across building systems:

  • HVAC ‚Äì adjusts airflow, heating, and cooling based on learned comfort zones instead of rigid set-points.
  • Lighting ‚Äì tunes illuminance and color temperature to match visual comfort and circadian rhythms.
  • Shading ‚Äì manages daylight and solar gain to avoid glare and overheating.

In all cases, the human remains in the loop: occupants see what the system is doing and can respond, building trust and transparency in AI-driven control.

4. People, Teaching & Inclusive Practice

Technology is only as strong as the people behind it. The program is built on collaboration, mentorship, and a commitment to diversity.

Human Impact

The research team brings together expertise from computer science, electrical engineering, environmental design, human-computer interaction, and behavioral science. The structure is deliberately collaborative rather than strictly hierarchical, encouraging open exchange of ideas.

  • Team members design algorithms, build prototypes, and run human-subject studies.
  • Partnerships with communities, schools, and industry allow real-world testing and feedback.
  • Students work directly on research problems, gaining experience that travels beyond the classroom.
